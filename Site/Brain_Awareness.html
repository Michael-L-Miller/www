Neuroscience Outreach

 

The brain is arguably the most complex organ. Its primary function is to direct behavior, and it determines the most appropriate behavior, either consciously or unconsciously, by integrating sensory inputs. It accomplishes this through a complex network of cells, that communicate electrically and electrochemically, to produce discrete, yet dynamic circuits. Therefore, as evidence of the brain's complexity, this organ is the physical substrate of the mind and gives rise to personality.

Although the brain is incredibly complicated, it is important for all persons to gain an understanding of the brain and the nervous system, since keeping a healthy brain is an essential component of maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

Binghamton University, New York

  1. -In line with this notion, the Neuroscience Club at Binghamton University, starting in 2009, developed campus- and community-wide programs during Brain Awareness Week (BAW) to promote nervous system education, and ways to keep a healthy brain and healthy body. Annually, every March, BAW is week-long international campaign sponsored by the the Dana Foundation and the Society for Neuroscience.

















Icahn School of Medicine at Mt. Sinai

  1. -Coinciding with BAW 2011, medical and graduate students from Mt. Sinai School of Medicine hosted Brain Day (2011), a one-day outreach event at a Manhattan public school.  Student groups organized activities that featured information about the brain, such as its function and organization, and how it relates to health.










  1. -Our booth highlighted sensation and perception, and to accomplish this, we focused on reflex arcs and stimuli discrimination. For instance, we demonstrated the relationship between olfaction and gustation with jelly beans. We also talked about the organization of the retina, and had students find their retinal blind spot. Moreover, using the popular mosquito ringtones, we illustrated how hearing changes as one ages.














  2. -A similar set of programs were developed for Brain Day the subsequent year (February 2012), once again highlighting vision, gustation, olfaction, audition and somatosensation. During these demonstrations, we gave a general overview of the body's sensory systems, mentioning that each system uses a distinct set of receptors to convert energy into meaningful neural signals.












  3. -We used this template - receptors, sensory organ & energy - to explain each sensory system. In the case of vision, for instance, we explained that the retina (sensory organ) contains rods and cones (receptors), and that these photoreceptors translate light waves (energy) into electrical signals that then travel to the brain.

  1. -Lauren Jarchin, Jaime Eberle, and I developed Brain Waves, a monthly newsletter published by the Club that focuses on a broad, yet interesting hot-topic in neuroscience. Club members than write articles related to the selected topic, and equally important, we invite a faculty member, with expertise in the selected topic, to submit an article. Broadly, members and readers are given a unique learning experience outside the classroom, but moreover, undergraduate learn about on-campus research opportunities from the faculty member's highlighted submission.
